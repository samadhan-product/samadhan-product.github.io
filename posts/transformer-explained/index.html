Transformer Explained Simply

Why Transformers feel difficult at first

Most people encounter Transformers through papers, diagrams, and math-heavy blog posts. The original paper title — "Attention Is All You Need" — is memorable but not exactly a friendly onboarding experience.

The difficulty usually comes from three places:

  • Stacked abstractions. Tokens, embeddings, attention, layers, heads, encoders, decoders — each builds on the last.
  • Unfamiliar vocabulary. Words like "Query" and "Key" sound like database jargon because the analogy actually helps — but nobody tells you that upfront.
  • Scale hides the idea. Production models have billions of parameters. It is easy to assume the architecture must be impossibly complex, when the core idea is surprisingly intuitive.

Product leader takeaway

You do not need to implement a Transformer to make good decisions about LLM products. You need to understand what it is good at (context, language, sequence patterns) and where it breaks (hallucination, ambiguity, cost at scale).

What is a Transformer?

A Transformer is a neural network architecture designed to process sequences — especially text — by letting every piece of the input interact with every other piece at once.

Before Transformers (roughly pre-2017), many language models used RNNs and LSTMs that read text left-to-right, one word at a time. That worked, but it was slow and struggled with long-range dependencies — like figuring out what "it" refers to ten words earlier.

Transformers changed the game by using attention instead of sequential reading. That is why modern LLMs (GPT, Claude, Gemini, Llama) are overwhelmingly Transformer-based.

Old approach (RNN/LSTM) Transformer approach
Reads sequentially, word by word Looks at all words in parallel
Struggles with distant context Direct connections between any two words
Hard to train at scale on GPUs Highly parallelizable — scales well

The core idea: attention

When you read a sentence, you do not treat every word equally. Your brain automatically weighs which words matter for understanding the current word.

"The animal did not cross the street because it was tired."

To understand "it," you instinctively look back at "animal" — not "street." Attention is the model's version of that instinct: a learned mechanism for deciding which other words to focus on.

One sentence summary

Attention answers: "For this word, which other words should I pay attention to?"

Tokens: how text becomes machine-readable

Models do not see words the way humans do. Text is split into tokens — chunks that might be whole words, parts of words, or punctuation.

Example

"I drink tea with ___" might become tokens like: ["I", " drink", " tea", " with", " ___"]

The blank (___) is where the model will predict the next token — perhaps "milk," "sugar," or "friends" depending on context learned during training.

Concept Plain English
Token A small unit of text the model processes
Tokenization Splitting raw text into those units
Vocabulary The set of all tokens the model knows
Context window Maximum number of tokens the model can handle at once

Token limits matter for product decisions. A 128K context window sounds large, but long documents, chat histories, and RAG payloads all consume tokens — and tokens cost money.

Embeddings: how words become numbers

Neural networks work with numbers, not strings. An embedding converts each token into a vector — a list of numbers that captures meaning and relationships.

Words with similar meaning end up closer together in this number-space. "King" and "Queen" sit near each other. "Dog" and "Cat" are neighbors. "King" and "Banana" are not.

Think of it this way

If tokens are the model's alphabet, embeddings are its dictionary — each entry is a rich numerical fingerprint of meaning, not just a label.

During training, the model adjusts these vectors so that useful patterns emerge. That is how "it" in one sentence can learn to point toward "animal" rather than "street."

Positional encoding: why word order matters

Attention looks at all words at once — which is powerful, but it also means the model loses the natural order of words unless we tell it explicitly.

"Dog bites man" vs "Man bites dog"

Same words. Completely different meaning. Positional encoding adds position information to each embedding so the model knows who did what to whom.

Without position With position
Bag of words — order lost Sequence preserved — subject/verb/object clear
"Dog bites man" ≈ "Man bites dog" Model distinguishes agent from patient

Modern models use variations of this idea (learned position embeddings, rotary embeddings, etc.), but the purpose is the same: word order is not optional in language.

Self-attention: how each word looks at other words

Self-attention means every token in a sentence attends to every other token in the same sentence — including itself.

For each word, the model computes attention scores: how relevant is every other word to understanding this one? High scores mean strong connection; low scores mean ignore.

Ambiguity example

"The doctor checked the patient because he was coughing."

Who is "he"? A human uses world knowledge. The model uses attention weights learned from millions of examples — typically linking "he" to "patient" in this pattern, though edge cases can still fail.

Another classic test:

"Rahul put the trophy in the suitcase because it was too big."

What does "it" refer to — the trophy or the suitcase? Humans know: trophies go inside suitcases when they are too big, not the other way around. Self-attention helps the model weigh "trophy" higher than "suitcase" for the word "it."

Query, Key, Value — explained with a search analogy

This is where many articles lose readers. The trick is to map Q/K/V to something you already understand: a search engine.

Component Search analogy In the Transformer
Query (Q) Your search question "I am the word it — what should I look at?"
Key (K) Index labels on documents "Here is how each word advertises itself for lookups"
Value (V) Actual content returned "Here is the information I pass along if selected"
  1. Each token creates a Query, Key, and Value vector from its embedding.
  2. Compare this token's Query against every token's Key → relevance scores.
  3. Scale and normalize those scores (softmax) so they sum to 100% attention.
  4. Blend all Value vectors using those weights → updated representation for this token.

Why three vectors?

Separating "what I'm looking for" (Query), "how I'm labeled" (Key), and "what I contribute" (Value) gives the model flexibility. The same word can ask different questions and offer different information in different contexts.

Multi-head attention: multiple viewpoints

One attention pass captures one type of relationship. Real language has many: grammar, coreference (what "it" means), topic, sentiment, and more.

Multi-head attention runs several attention operations in parallel — each "head" learns a different lens. One head might track subject-verb agreement; another might focus on pronoun antecedents.

Single-head Multi-head (e.g. 8–12 heads)
One relationship pattern Many patterns simultaneously
Limited expressiveness Richer, more nuanced representations

The outputs are concatenated and projected back — giving each token a representation informed by multiple types of context at once.

Feed-forward network: thinking after looking

Attention gathers context. The feed-forward network (FFN) processes what was gathered — like reflecting after you have read the relevant parts of a document.

Each token passes through a small two-layer neural network independently. This step adds non-linearity and depth: the model does not just mix information, it transforms it.

Attention + FFN = one block

A Transformer layer typically stacks: Attention → FFN, with residual connections and normalization between them. Repeat this block many times (12, 24, 96+ layers depending on model size).

Encoder and decoder

The original Transformer had two halves:

  • Encoder — reads and understands the input. Builds rich representations of every token.
  • Decoder — generates output one token at a time, using what the encoder understood (in seq2seq tasks like translation).
Architecture Used for Examples
Encoder-only Understanding / classification BERT
Decoder-only Text generation GPT, Llama, Claude
Encoder-decoder Input → output transformation Translation (T5, BART)

Most chat LLMs you use today are decoder-only: they predict the next token given everything before it. That is why prompting matters — you are literally shaping the input sequence the decoder conditions on.

Decoder masking: no cheating while predicting text

When training a decoder to generate text, the model must predict word 5 using only words 1–4 — not words 6–10. Otherwise it cheats by peeking at the answer.

Causal masking (or look-ahead masking) hides future tokens during training. Each position can only attend to itself and earlier positions.

Why this matters for products

At inference time, the model genuinely does not know what it will say next — it generates one token at a time. That is why streaming responses work token-by-token, and why early tokens in a completion constrain everything that follows.

Final softmax: choosing the next token

After all layers process the input, the model produces a score for every token in its vocabulary. Softmax converts those scores into probabilities that sum to 100%.

The model then picks the next token — usually the highest probability, but temperature and sampling add controlled randomness for more natural output.

"I drink tea with ___"

Token candidate Illustrative probability
milk42%
sugar28%
lemon15%
friends8%
…others7%

Probabilities are illustrative — real distributions depend on training data and context. The key insight: LLMs are next-token prediction machines, scaled until emergent abilities appear useful.

Complete example: walking through one sentence

"The animal did not cross the street because it was tired."

Here is how a Transformer-style model roughly processes this sentence:

  1. Tokenize — Split into tokens: "The", " animal", " did", " not", " cross", " the", " street", " because", " it", " was", " tired", "."
  2. Embed — Convert each token to a numerical vector; add positional encoding so order is preserved.
  3. Self-attention layers — When processing "it", attention heads compare its Query to Keys from all other tokens. "Animal" gets a high weight; "street" gets a low weight.
  4. Multi-head blend — Several heads may confirm the coreference link while others capture grammar or negation ("did not cross").
  5. Feed-forward — Each token's vector is refined based on the contextual blend from attention.
  6. Repeat — Stack 12–100+ layers; representations get increasingly abstract and task-aware.
  7. Output (if generating) — For prediction tasks, the final layer's representation of the last position feeds softmax over the vocabulary for the next token.

What you just learned

The model did not look up a rule that "it" means "animal." It learned statistical patterns across billions of examples so attention weights favor the right connection. That is powerful — and also why edge cases and hallucinations still happen.

Transformer Neural architecture for sequences; uses attention instead of sequential RNN reading.
Token Smallest text unit the model processes; drives cost and context limits.
Embedding Numeric vector representing token meaning in high-dimensional space.

Self-attention Each word weighs every other word to build contextual understanding.

Decoder Generates output token by token (GPT-style).
